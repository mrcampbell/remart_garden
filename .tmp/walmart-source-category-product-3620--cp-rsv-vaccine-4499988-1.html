Important RSV information

What is respiratory syncytial virus (RSV)?

Respiratory syncytial virus, or RSV, is a common respiratory virus that usually causes mild, cold-like symptoms. Most people recover in a week or two, but RSV can be serious, especially for older adults, resulting in shortness of breath and low oxygen levels.

Those at high risk for severe illness from RSV include:

• Older adults, especially those 60 years & older

• Adults with chronic lung or heart disease

• Adults with weakened immune systems

Learn more at: https://www.cdc.gov/vaccines/vpd/rsv/index.html

What is the RSV vaccine?

RSV vaccines help our bodies develop immunity to the virus that causes RSV, without us having to get the illness. While each RSV vaccine provides protection against the virus, their effects on our bodies may vary.

When should I get an RSV vaccine?

The timing of RSV season can vary, but infections tend to be most common from September to January.

Who should get the RSV vaccine?

• People at higher risk of suffering from severe RSV include patients with lung diseases, such as COPD or asthma; those with heart disease, diabetes, or those with kidney or liver disorders; and those who are immunocompromised, or who have other risk factors

• Adults 60 years and older should have a discussion with their pharmacist or healthcare provider to determine if the RSV vaccine is right for them

• People who are 32-36 weeks pregnant during RSV season

Why should you get the RSV vaccine?

The RSV vaccination may prevent illnesses, medical visits, hospitalization & death. It’s an important preventive tool for people with chronic health conditions or a weakened immune system, as well as those who are 32-36 weeks pregnant during RSV season.

RSV Vaccine FAQ

How much does the RSV vaccine cost?

The RSV vaccine may be available at no cost to you through your insurance benefits. Refer to your plan for details.

What are the side effects of the RSV vaccine?

According to clinical trials, the most common side effects associated with the RSV vaccine were fatigue, headache, pain at the injection site, and muscle pain.

Is the RSV vaccine FDA approved?

Yes, there are currently two FDA-approved vaccines for the prevention of lower respiratory tract disease caused by RSV, with more options coming to market soon.
